Introduction

Haloalkanes, also known as alkyl halides, are organic compounds in which one or more hydrogen atoms in an alkane are replaced by halogen atoms (F, Cl, Br, I). They are generally represented by the formula R–X, where R is an alkyl group and X is a halogen.

Haloalkanes are important intermediates in organic synthesis and find applications in industry, pharmaceuticals, and as solvents.

Classification

  • Based on the number of halogen atoms:

    • Monohaloalkanes: Contain one halogen atom (e.g., CH₃Cl).

    • Dihaloalkanes: Contain two halogen atoms (e.g., CH₂Cl₂).

    • Polyhaloalkanes: Contain more than two halogen atoms (e.g., CCl₄).

  • Based on carbon atom attached to halogen:

    • Primary (1°) haloalkanes: Halogen attached to primary carbon (e.g., CH₃CH₂Cl).

    • Secondary (2°) haloalkanes: Halogen attached to secondary carbon (e.g., CH₃CHClCH₃).

    • Tertiary (3°) haloalkanes: Halogen attached to tertiary carbon (e.g., (CH₃)₃CCl).

  • Based on type of halogen: Fluoroalkanes, Chloroalkanes, Bromoalkanes, Iodoalkanes.

Preparation Methods

  • From Alkanes: Free radical halogenation using Cl₂ or Br₂ in the presence of UV light or heat.

  • From Alcohols:

    • Reaction with hydrogen halides: R–OH + HX → R–X + H₂O

    • Using phosphorus halides: PCl₃, PCl₅, or SOCl₂

  • From Alkenes: Addition of halogens or hydrogen halides to double bonds.

  • From Grignard Reagents: RMgX + HX → R–X + MgX₂

Physical Properties

  • Generally colorless liquids (some are solids) with characteristic odors.

  • Boiling points increase with molecular weight and presence of heavier halogens.

  • Density is higher than water due to the halogen atom.

  • Insoluble in water but soluble in organic solvents like ether, benzene, or alcohol.

Chemical Properties

  • Nucleophilic Substitution (SN1 & SN2): Haloalkanes react with nucleophiles like OH⁻, CN⁻, NH₃ to form alcohols, nitriles, and amines.

  • Elimination (Dehydrohalogenation): Reaction with alcoholic KOH produces alkenes.

  • Reduction: Haloalkanes can be reduced to alkanes using metals and hydrogen.

  • Reaction with Metals: Forms organometallic compounds, e.g., RMgX (Grignard reagents) and RLi (organolithium compounds).

  • Reaction with Silver Nitrate (AgNO₃): Used to test the reactivity of haloalkanes. Alkyl halide + AgNO₃ → AgX + R–OH

Important Notes

  • Reactivity order for halogens in haloalkanes: RI > RBr > RCl > RF.

  • SN1 reactions are favored by tertiary haloalkanes due to carbocation stability.

  • SN2 reactions are favored by primary haloalkanes due to steric accessibility.

  • Haloalkanes are toxic and should be handled with care.

Uses

  • Used as solvents in paints, varnishes, and industrial processes.

  • Intermediate in pharmaceutical and agrochemical synthesis.

  • Used in the production of polymers like PVC (from chloroethane).

  • Used as refrigerants, propellants, and fumigants.
