Introduction

Organic halides are organic compounds containing one or more halogen atoms (fluorine, chlorine, bromine, or iodine) bonded to carbon atoms. They are classified into two main categories:

  • Haloalkanes (Alkyl Halides): Halogen atoms attached to sp3 hybridized carbon atoms.

  • Haloarenes (Aryl Halides): Halogen atoms attached to sp2 hybridized carbon atoms in an aromatic ring.

Physical Properties

  • Boiling Points: Organic halides generally have higher boiling points than alkanes of similar molecular weight due to the presence of polar C–X bonds.

  • Solubility: They are typically insoluble in water but soluble in organic solvents like ether and alcohol.

  • Density: Most organic halides are denser than water.

Chemical Properties

  • Reactivity: Organic halides undergo nucleophilic substitution reactions (SN1 and SN2 mechanisms) and elimination reactions (E1 and E2 mechanisms).

  • Hydrolysis: They can hydrolyze to form alcohols or phenols.

  • Reduction: Reduction of alkyl halides leads to the formation of alkanes.

  • Wurtz Reaction: Alkyl halides can undergo coupling reactions in the presence of sodium metal to form alkanes.

Applications

  • Solvents: Used as solvents in various industrial processes.

  • Pharmaceuticals: Serve as intermediates in the synthesis of drugs.

  • Pesticides: Some halogenated compounds are used as pesticides.

  • Reagents: Employed in organic synthesis reactions.

Note: While organic halides have numerous applications, some are toxic and environmentally hazardous. Proper handling and disposal are essential.
