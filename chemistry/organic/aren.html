Introduction

Aromatic hydrocarbons, or arenes, are organic compounds containing one or more benzene rings. They are characterized by their unique stability due to delocalized π-electrons and are widely used in various industries.

Key Properties

  • Planar ring structure with sp² hybridized carbon atoms.

  • Delocalized π-electrons over the ring, contributing to stability.

  • Undergo electrophilic substitution reactions rather than addition reactions.

  • Generally non-polar and insoluble in water.

Examples of Aromatic Hydrocarbons

  • Benzene (C₆H₆) – The simplest aromatic compound, used as a solvent and precursor in chemical synthesis.

  • Toluene (C₆H₅CH₃) – Used as a solvent and in the production of various chemicals.

  • Aniline (C₆H₅NH₂) – An amine derivative of benzene, important in dye manufacturing.

  • Phenol (C₆H₅OH) – Used in the production of plastics, disinfectants, and pharmaceuticals.

Reactions of Aromatic Hydrocarbons

  • Electrophilic Substitution: A hydrogen atom on the benzene ring is replaced by an electrophile.

  • Friedel–Crafts Alkylation and Acylation: Introduction of alkyl or acyl groups to the aromatic ring.

  • Halogenation: Substitution of a hydrogen atom with a halogen atom in the presence of a catalyst.

Note: Aromatic hydrocarbons are essential in various chemical processes and the production of numerous industrial chemicals.

Applications

  • Solvents in paints, coatings, and adhesives.

  • Precursors in the synthesis of plastics, synthetic fibers, and rubbers.

  • Intermediate compounds in the manufacture of pharmaceuticals and agrochemicals.
