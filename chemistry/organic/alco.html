Introduction

Alcohols are organic compounds containing one or more hydroxyl (-OH) groups attached to a carbon atom. They are classified based on the number of hydroxyl groups and the nature of the carbon to which the -OH group is attached.

Classification of Alcohols

  • Monohydric Alcohols: Contain one -OH group (e.g., Methanol, Ethanol).

  • Polyhydric Alcohols: Contain more than one -OH group (e.g., Ethylene glycol, Glycerol).

    • Monohydric Alcohols Classification

    • Primary Alcohols: The -OH group is attached to a carbon bonded to one other carbon (e.g., Ethanol).

    • Secondary Alcohols: The -OH group is attached to a carbon bonded to two other carbons (e.g., Isopropanol).

    • Tertiary Alcohols: The -OH group is attached to a carbon bonded to three other carbons (e.g., Tert-butyl alcohol).

Preparation of Alcohols

  • From Haloalkanes: Reacting haloalkanes with aqueous NaOH or KOH yields alcohols.

  • From Primary Amines: Amines react with nitrous acid to form alcohols.

  • From Esters: Hydrolysis or reduction of esters produces alcohols.

  • Industrial Methods: Alcohols can be prepared through processes like the oxo process, hydroboration-oxidation of alkenes, and fermentation of sugars.

Physical Properties

  • Alcohols are colorless liquids or solids at room temperature.

  • They have higher boiling points than hydrocarbons due to hydrogen bonding.

  • Lower molecular weight alcohols are miscible with water; solubility decreases with increasing molecular weight.

Chemical Properties

  • Reaction with HX: Alcohols react with hydrogen halides to form alkyl halides.

  • Reaction with PX₃, PCl₅, SOCl₂: Converts alcohols to alkyl halides.

  • Reaction with Reactive Metals: Alcohols react with metals like Na, K, and Li to release hydrogen gas.

  • Dehydration: Alcohols undergo dehydration to form alkenes.

  • Oxidation: Primary alcohols oxidize to aldehydes and then to carboxylic acids; secondary alcohols oxidize to ketones; tertiary alcohols are resistant to oxidation under normal conditions.

  • Esterification: Alcohols react with carboxylic acids to form esters.

Distinction of Alcohol Types

The Victor Meyer method is used to distinguish between primary, secondary, and tertiary alcohols based on the color change observed during the reaction sequence involving phosphorus triiodide (PI₃), silver nitrite (AgNO₃), and nitrous acid.

Applications

  • Solvents: Alcohols are widely used as solvents in various chemical processes.

  • Fuel: Ethanol is used as a fuel or fuel additive.

  • Medicinal: Alcohols like ethanol and isopropanol are used as antiseptics and disinfectants.

  • Industrial: Alcohols are involved in the synthesis of plastics, pharmaceuticals, and other chemicals.
