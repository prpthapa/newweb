Introduction

Phenols are organic compounds characterized by a hydroxyl group (-OH) attached to an aromatic ring. The simplest phenol is hydroxybenzene (C₆H₆OH), commonly known as phenol or carbolic acid.

Structure and Nomenclature

  • Phenols consist of a benzene ring bonded to a hydroxyl group.

  • The IUPAC name is "benzenol," but "phenol" is widely accepted.

  • Substituted phenols are named based on the position of the substituent relative to the hydroxyl group.

Physical Properties

  • Phenols are weak acids, more acidic than alcohols due to resonance stabilization of the phenoxide ion.

  • They are slightly soluble in water and have a characteristic odor.

  • Phenols can form hydrogen bonds, influencing their boiling and melting points.

Chemical Properties

  • Phenols undergo electrophilic aromatic substitution reactions, such as halogenation, nitration, and sulfonation.

  • They can form esters with acyl chlorides and ethers with alkyl halides.

  • Oxidation of phenols can lead to the formation of quinones.

Uses of Phenols

  • Phenols are used in the production of plastics, such as Bakelite.

  • They serve as intermediates in the synthesis of pharmaceuticals and agrochemicals.

  • Phenolic compounds act as antioxidants and antimicrobial agents in various applications.

Note: Phenols play a crucial role in organic chemistry due to their unique reactivity and diverse applications.
