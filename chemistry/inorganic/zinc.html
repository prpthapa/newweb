Introduction

Zinc (Zn) is a bluish-white, lustrous metal with atomic number 30 and atomic mass 65.38 u. It belongs to Group 12 of the periodic table and is classified as a transition metal. Zinc is relatively abundant in the Earth's crust and occurs naturally in minerals such as sphalerite (ZnS), smithsonite (ZnCO₃), and zincite (ZnO). It is an essential trace element in the human body, playing a vital role in enzyme function, DNA synthesis, and immune system regulation.

Physical Properties

  • Appearance: Bluish-white, shiny metal with a brittle texture at room temperature.

  • Melting Point: 419.5°C, which is relatively low for metals.

  • Boiling Point: 907°C.

  • Density: 7.14 g/cm³.

  • Crystal Structure: Hexagonal close-packed (hcp) arrangement.

  • Conductivity: Good conductor of electricity but less than copper and silver.

Chemical Properties

  • Reactivity: Zinc is fairly reactive; it reacts with oxygen to form zinc oxide (ZnO) and with halogens to form zinc halides (e.g., ZnCl₂).

  • Reaction with Acids: Zinc reacts with dilute acids such as HCl to produce hydrogen gas (Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂↑).

  • Reaction with Alkalis: Zinc reacts with strong bases like NaOH to form zincates (e.g., Zn + 2NaOH → Na₂[Zn(OH)₄]).

  • Corrosion Resistance: Zinc forms a protective oxide layer that prevents further corrosion; this property is utilized in galvanization.

  • Allotropic Forms: Zinc shows a metallic crystalline form with no allotropes at room temperature, but its vapor phase contains diatomic zinc molecules (Zn₂).

Occurrence and Extraction

  • Zinc is obtained mainly from the mineral sphalerite (ZnS) through roasting to zinc oxide and subsequent reduction with carbon or electrolysis.

  • Other ores include zinc carbonate (smithsonite) and zinc silicate.

  • The extraction process involves purification to obtain high-purity zinc metal for industrial use.

Applications

  • Galvanization: Zinc coating on iron or steel prevents rust and increases longevity.

  • Alloys: Used in brass (copper + zinc), bronze, and other corrosion-resistant alloys.

  • Battery Technology: Zinc-carbon and zinc-air batteries rely on the electrochemical properties of zinc.

  • Chemical Industry: Zinc oxide is used in rubber, paints, ceramics, and cosmetics; zinc sulfide is used in phosphorescent materials.

  • Biological Role: Zinc is essential for enzymes such as carbonic anhydrase and plays a role in wound healing, growth, and immunity.

  • Other Uses: Zinc compounds are used as preservatives, pigments, and in pharmaceuticals like zinc sulfate supplements.

Health and Safety

  • Zinc is non-toxic in moderate quantities but excessive exposure can lead to metal fume fever.

  • Zinc compounds should be handled carefully in powdered form due to flammability and inhalation hazards.

  • Zinc deficiency in humans can cause stunted growth, weakened immunity, and skin disorders.

Note: Zinc is a versatile metal with widespread industrial and biological importance. Its ability to resist corrosion, form useful alloys, and participate in biological processes makes it a critical element in both technology and life sciences.
