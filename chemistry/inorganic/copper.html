Introduction

Copper (Cu) is a chemical element with the atomic number 29. It is a reddish-brown metal known for its high thermal and electrical conductivity, second only to silver. Copper occurs naturally in the Earth's crust, primarily in minerals like chalcopyrite, bornite, and malachite, and is extracted through mining and smelting processes. Historically, it was one of the first metals used by humans, dating back over 10,000 years, and played a key role in the development of tools, ornaments, and coinage.

Physical Properties

  • Appearance: Reddish-brown metal with a metallic luster.

  • Density: 8.96 g/cm³.

  • Melting Point: 1,084.62°C (1,984.32°F).

  • Boiling Point: 2,560°C (4,640°F).

  • Electrical Conductivity: Excellent conductor of electricity.

  • Thermal Conductivity: High thermal conductivity.

  • Corrosion Resistance: Forms a protective oxide layer when exposed to air, known as patina.

Chemical Properties

  • Oxidation States: Primarily +1 (cuprous) and +2 (cupric).

  • Reaction with Oxygen: Reacts with oxygen to form copper oxides (CuO and Cu₂O).

  • Reaction with Acids: Reacts with oxidizing acids like nitric acid to form copper salts.

  • Formation of Alloys: Combines with metals like tin to form bronze and with zinc to form brass.

Applications

  • Electrical Wiring: Due to its excellent electrical conductivity, copper is widely used in electrical wiring.

  • Plumbing: Copper pipes are commonly used in plumbing systems.

  • Construction: Used in roofing materials and architectural elements.

  • Alloys: Forms alloys like bronze and brass, which have various industrial and decorative applications.

  • Electronics: Utilized in the manufacturing of electronic components.

Biological Role

Copper is essential to all aerobic organisms. It is particularly associated with oxygen metabolism. For example, it is found in the respiratory enzyme complex cytochrome c oxidase, in the oxygen-carrying hemocyanin, and in several hydroxylases. Adult humans contain between 1.4 and 2.1 mg of copper per kilogram of body weight. Copper is involved in various enzymatic processes and plays a crucial role in iron metabolism and the formation of hemoglobin.

Health and Safety

  • Deficiency: Copper deficiency can lead to anemia, neutropenia, and bone abnormalities.

  • Toxicity: Excessive copper intake can cause liver damage and gastrointestinal distress.

  • Recommended Intake: The recommended dietary allowance (RDA) for copper varies by age and sex, but for adults, it is approximately 900 micrograms per day.

Note: Copper is a versatile metal with widespread industrial and biological importance. Its ability to resist corrosion, form useful alloys, and participate in biological processes makes it a critical element in both technology and life sciences.
