Interference

Interference is the phenomenon that occurs when two or more light waves overlap and combine to form a new wave pattern. The resulting intensity at any point depends on the phase difference between the waves: when waves arrive in phase they produce constructive interference (bright fringes), and when they arrive out of phase they produce destructive interference (dark fringes).

Young’s Double Slit Experiment

Young’s double slit experiment demonstrates interference using two coherent light sources. Light passing through two closely spaced slits forms alternate bright and dark bands on a screen. The fringe spacing depends on the wavelength of light, the slit separation, and the distance to the screen.

Key Points

  • Interference confirms the wave nature of light.

  • Constructive interference: phase difference = 0, 2π, 4π, ...

  • Destructive interference: phase difference = π, 3π, 5π, ...

  • Fringe width ∝ wavelength and screen distance, and ∝ 1 / slit separation.

Applications

Interference is used in thin-film colours, anti-reflection coatings, interferometers for precise measurements, and in analyzing optical surfaces. It also explains colourful patterns seen in soap bubbles and oil films.
