Capacitance

Capacitance is the property of a system (usually two conductors separated by an insulator or vacuum) to store electric charge when a potential difference is applied. It tells us how much charge can be stored per unit voltage and is a key concept in electrostatics and circuit theory.

Definition and Unit

The capacitance C of a conductor (or a pair of conductors) is defined as:

C = Q / V

where Q is the magnitude of charge stored and V is the voltage (potential difference) between the conductors. The SI unit of capacitance is the farad (F), which means one coulomb of charge stored with one volt applied.

Factors Affecting Capacitance

For two parallel-plate conductors (a common model), capacitance depends on:

  • The surface area A of the plates – larger area gives higher capacitance.

  • The distance d between the plates – smaller separation gives higher capacitance.

  • The dielectric material between the plates – a material with higher relative permittivity εᵣ increases capacitance.

The formula for a parallel-plate capacitor in free space (or vacuum) becomes:

C = ε₀ εᵣ (A / d)

where ε₀ is the permittivity of free space and εᵣ is the dielectric constant of the material between the plates.

Energy Stored in a Charged Capacitor

When a capacitor is charged to a voltage V and a charge Q, work is done to move the charges. The energyU stored in the electric field between the plates is given by:

U = ½ C V² = ½ Q V = Q² / (2 C)

This energy can be released when the capacitor discharges, and the stored energy concept is fundamental in many applications such as flash circuits, filters and energy storage systems.

Capacitors in Series and Parallel

In practical circuits we often connect capacitors together. The rules are:

  • Series connection: The reciprocal of the total capacitance is the sum of reciprocals of individual capacitances. Voltage divides among capacitors.

  • Parallel connection: The total capacitance is the sum of the individual capacitances. They share the same voltage but store charge independently.

Dielectric and Its Effects

A dielectric is an insulating material placed between the plates of a capacitor. Its presence affects the electric field and increases the capacitance. The effects include:

  • Reduction of the effective field inside the capacitor, allowing more charge for the same voltage.

  • Increase of the dielectric constant εᵣ, which enters the capacitance formula.

  • Influence on maximum voltage rating (dielectric strength) and hence safe usage of capacitor in circuits.

Key Applications

Capacitors and capacitance have wide-ranging uses in modern physics and engineering:

  • Energy storage for short-term bursts (camera flash, backup power).

  • Filtering and smoothing of voltage in power supplies.

  • Tuning and resonant circuits in radios and oscillators.

  • Coupling and decoupling in electronic circuits to allow AC signals while blocking DC.

Summary

Capacitance is a fundamental concept bridging electrostatics and circuit theory. By understanding how charge and voltage relate, how geometry and materials affect storage, and how energy is stored and released, students build a foundation for advanced topics like dielectrics, alternating current circuits, and modern electronics. Mastering this concept opens the way to deeper study of how electrical energy is controlled and used in devices around us.
