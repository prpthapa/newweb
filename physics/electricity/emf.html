Electromotive Force (EMF)

Electromotive force (EMF) is a fundamental concept in electrical and modern physics. It refers to the work done per unit charge by a source (such as a battery or generator) in bringing charge around a complete circuit, including any internal resistance within the source. In simpler terms, EMF is the maximum potential difference the source can provide when no current is flowing.

Definition and SI Unit

Formally, EMF (denoted by ε) is defined as the energy supplied by the source per coulomb of charge:
ε = W / q
where W is work done and q is the charge. The SI unit of EMF is the volt (V), the same as for potential difference.

Internal Resistance of a Source

In real sources like batteries and generators, there is always some internal resistance (denoted r) inside the device. This means the terminal voltage (V) delivered when current (I) flows is less than the ideal EMF:

V = ε − I r

where V is the terminal voltage, ε is the EMF of the source, I is the current flowing, and r is the internal resistance. When no current flows (open-circuit), V = ε.

Series and Parallel Combinations of Sources

When multiple sources (batteries or cells) are connected:

  • Series connection: EMFs add up, internal resistances also add:
    εₜ = ε₁ + ε₂ + …,  rₜ = r₁ + r₂ + …

  • Parallel connection: For identical sources:
    εₜ = ε₁ = ε₂ = …,  rₜ = (r₁ / n) when n identical cells are in parallel.

Power Supplied and Internal Work Done

The power supplied by the source's EMF is
Pₛ = ε × I
Some of this power is dissipated inside the source due to its internal resistance:
Pᵢ = I² r
The remaining power is delivered to the external circuit:
Pₒ = V × I = (ε − I r) × I

Importance and Applications

Understanding EMF is essential for analysing real circuits, predicting performance of batteries and power supplies, and designing reliable electrical systems. Applications include:

  • Battery and cell design — estimating how much terminal voltage will drop under load.

  • Generator and alternator design — relating mechanical work to electrical output.

  • Electrical diagnostics — assessing the health of a source via internal resistance measurement.

  • Renewable energy systems — modelling how solar-cells or fuel-cells act as EMF sources with internal losses.

Summary

In summary, electromotive force is a key concept that bridges the ideal world of perfect voltage sources and the practical realities of internal resistance and voltage drop. By introducing EMF, internal resistance, and real current-delivered voltages, students gain a comprehensive understanding of how sources behave in circuits. This understanding is foundational for progressing into topics such as battery charging/discharging, alternators, and power management in modern electrical systems.
